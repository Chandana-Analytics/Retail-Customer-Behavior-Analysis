\# Recommendations



\## 1. Give greater attention to customers purchasing only once or occasionally.



The largest share of customers consisted of One-time Buyers and Occasional Buyers. Since these customers already completed at least one purchase, understanding why they did not purchase more frequently could help the business identify opportunities to increase repeat buying.



\## 2. Protect the customers who contribute the most revenue.



Spending analysis showed that only a small group of customers generated substantially higher revenue than the rest of the customer base. Any noticeable decline in purchasing activity from these customers could have a direct impact on overall revenue, making regular monitoring of this group important.



\## 3. Avoid measuring customer value by purchase quantity alone.



Purchase volume and spending analyses showed that customers buying the largest quantities were not always the highest revenue contributors. Customer evaluations should therefore consider both purchase quantity and revenue to provide a more balanced assessment of customer value.



\## 4. Investigate the reasons behind customer inactivity.



Recency analysis and RFM segmentation identified Lost Customers as the largest customer segment. Rather than treating this only as a marketing issue, the business should first understand why these customers stopped purchasing before deciding on appropriate retention actions.



\## 5. Apply different business approaches to different customer segments.



RFM segmentation showed clear differences in customer behavior across Champions, Loyal Customers, Potential Loyalists, Big Spenders, At Risk, Lost, and Regular Customers. Treating every customer the same may overlook these differences. Customer segments should be used as a reference when planning future customer management strategies.
